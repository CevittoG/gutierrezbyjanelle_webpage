## Users

Two equally important audiences from Janelle's side — the design work is the same craft regardless of occasion. From a marketing posture, weddings lead because they have the longest planning cycle, the highest emotional stakes, and the strongest referral velocity; events follow, never as a footnote.

**Wedding audience (the marketing lead):**

- **The researcher** — newly-engaged, browsing vendors for the first time. Looking for inspiration and a feeling they can trust. Mostly on a phone, often in fragmented sessions (lunch break, evenings, while showing a partner).
- **The evaluator** — narrowed down to a shortlist, comparing fit and pricing. Wants to confirm the work is real, the process is clear, and reaching out won't be awkward.
- **The referred lead** — heard about Janelle from a friend or saw her on Zola/Instagram. Already warm; needs the site to confirm the recommendation and offer the easiest possible next step.

**Event audience (graduations, baby showers, birthdays, anniversaries, quinceañeras, retirement parties, etc.):**

- **The host** — planning a celebration that isn't a wedding but matters just as much. Often a parent, family member, or close friend organizing for someone else. Wants stationery and signage that feels custom, not template-shop.
- **The returning client** — already worked with Janelle on a wedding or earlier event and is now coming back for the next milestone. Trust is already there; they need the site to confirm Janelle handles non-wedding occasions with the same care.

Family members (especially mothers and grandmothers, some bilingual Spanish/English) often visit alongside the primary planner — the site needs to read clearly on a 60-year-old's phone in bright daylight, not just on a designer's laptop.

## Product Purpose

Showcase Janelle's custom wedding *and* event stationery in a way that makes the craft feel obvious, the voice feel like hers, and the next step feel easy. Weddings are the strategic marketing lead — longer planning cycles, deeper budgets, stronger referral velocity — but events (graduations, baby showers, birthdays, quinceañeras, anniversaries) are the same craft and the same business; the site must never treat them as a B-side. Success is measured across four outcomes, not one:

1. Direct inquiry (Instagram DM, email, contact form).
2. Etsy purchase of ready-to-customize digital products.
3. Booking through the Zola vendor profile.
4. Brand affinity — saved, bookmarked, or shared with a partner so Janelle stays top-of-mind through a long planning cycle.

The site is the portfolio, the storefront entry, and the first impression — design IS the product.

## Brand Personality

**Intimate. Refined. Tasteful.**

Warmth comes from voice and care, not from script fonts and heart bullets. Refinement comes from restraint — fewer, better choices, never a Pinterest collage. The site should feel like Janelle talking directly to one couple, the way her own copy already does ("Talk soon… xx").

Tone: first-person, gentle, confident in the craft without performing it. Celebrates love without sentimentality. Treats the reader like a peer, not a customer.

## Anti-references

Explicitly NOT any of these:

- **Generic Etsy seller template.** Stock script font, flat tan background, heart bullets, "boho chic" stock photography. The default wedding-crafter look. We are not that.
- **Corporate SaaS landing.** Gradient hero, three-up feature cards, "Trusted by" logo strip, big-CTA-button-on-pastel. Sterile and category-coded for software, not stationery.
- **Pinterest-board chaos.** Five fonts, ten photos, no hierarchy, sparkles everywhere. Overstimulating; signals amateur.
- **Cold luxury / minimal-to-a-fault.** Vogue-style white-space-as-status, type so small it whispers. Too distant for a warm, custom business built on personal relationships.

The work and the voice should always read as the loudest things on the page; styling should disappear behind them.

## Design Principles

1. **The work speaks first.** Photography and real stationery are the heroes on every page. Copy, CTAs, and chrome stay quieter than the craft. If a design element competes with a photo, the design element loses.
2. **Intimate, not romantic.** Warmth is earned through tone and detail — Janelle's own words, careful spacing, considered typography — not through script-font shortcuts or heart-emoji decoration. The accent cursive is a guest, not the host.
3. **Restraint is the tell of taste.** Fewer tiers, fewer fonts, fewer flourishes than competitors. Confidence through what's left out. Every element on a page should be defensible.
4. **Bilingual by design.** Spanish and English are both first-class. Layouts must survive longer Spanish strings; tone should feel native in either language; a Spanish-speaking mother-of-the-groom should never feel like a footnote.
5. **Every page earns the inquiry.** Home, Gallery, Reviews, Weddings, Investment — each one is a step toward "I want to talk to Janelle." No page is a dead end; no page begs.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

- **WCAG 2.1 AA** as the floor for contrast, focus, hit targets, and keyboard navigation. Already partway there (skip-to-content link, `prefers-reduced-motion`, focus-visible rings) — hold the line on new work.
- **Multilingual (English + Spanish).** Reviews already mix both. Layouts must accommodate longer Spanish strings without truncation. When new copy is added, treat translatability as a constraint, not an afterthought.
- **Mobile-first and daylight-readable.** Most visits come from phones, often outdoors at a venue tour or in bright cafés. Body type stays readable on a 60-year-old's phone in sun; contrast holds up on warm taupe backgrounds (the current palette is close to its readability floor — be careful when reducing it further).
- **Reduced-motion respected globally** — already wired in `globals.css`. Any new animation must honor it.
